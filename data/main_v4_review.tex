\title{The ($\rho$, $\Pi$) Bridge to Gravitational-Wave Inference: \\
Commutator Geometry of Exact Posterior Trajectories and the Bias of
Sequential Gaussian Filters in LIGO}

\author{Skye Laflamme}
\affiliation{RavenNest Science, Santa Cruz, CA, USA}

\date{June 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We present the ($\rho$, $\Pi$) bridge: a formal connection between the
geometry of sequential Bayesian inference in gravitational-wave astronomy
and the commutator structure of the Fisher information matrix as it evolves
across the detector band. The central claim is that the ($\rho$, $\Pi$)
dynamics describe the evolution of the posterior distribution over binary
orbital parameters as data accumulates, not the orbital dynamics of the
binary itself. This is an epistemological claim: the ($\rho$, $\Pi$)
formalism describes \emph{how we come to know} the binary, not the binary's
own physics.

We distinguish two objects of study that are often conflated. The
\textbf{exact posterior trajectory} $\rho_f$ (the one-parameter family of
posteriors given data below cutoff $f$) is order-independent by Bayes'
theorem; its geometry is an exact property of the inference problem. The
\textbf{single-pass Gaussian filter} (sequential Laplace approximation
across frequency bands) is order-dependent and carries a deterministic bias
relative to the batch posterior; characterizing that bias is the second
subject of the paper. Both are governed by the same quantity: the commutator
$[\Gamma, \Pi]$ between the cumulative Fisher matrix and the projector onto
its best-constrained direction.

\textbf{Static evidence:} We reframe the well-known GW170817 posterior
structure---two analyses with different spin priors probing a continuous
degeneracy ridge in $(q, \chi_{\text{eff}}, \tilde{\Lambda})$---as a test of
prior lensing: the Fisher inverse amplifies any prior perturbation into the
degeneracy direction $\vd$, predicting a displacement $\Delta x \parallel
\vd$ regardless of perturbation details. The alignment is assessed against a
pre-registered random-direction null [$p = \manifest{T3.pval}$]. The test does
\emph{not} reject the null ($p > 0.05$): the measured displacement is
consistent with a random direction in the four-dimensional subspace, and the
prediction of Lemma~1 is not confirmed by this dataset. We discuss possible
explanations (hard-truncation priors violating the smooth-perturbation
assumption; metric dependence of the alignment test (the Fisher inner product suppresses the ridge direction)). GW150914 provides
a separate null test: its smaller Fisher condition number predicts weaker
prior-induced displacement per unit prior perturbation.

\textbf{Dynamic evidence:} A Fisher information sweep across the band
20--500~Hz yields growth of the commutator measure $C(f)$ by a factor
of approximately $\manifest{T6.growth_factor}$ (primary fiducial),

with piecewise slopes that vary from ${\sim}1.7$ at low frequencies to
${\sim}0.01$ near 500~Hz, reflecting the post-Newtonian transition bands.
The variation across analytic and measured PSDs is a factor of
$\manifest{T8.max_variation}$, which exceeds the pre-registered
$10\%$ falsification threshold; we discuss the implications in
Sec.~\ref{sec:dynamic}. The qualitative growth---rather than any single
numerical value---is the robust feature.

\textbf{Forward model:} The discriminating signal-to-noise
$\rhosq(f) = \Delta x^\top\Gamma(f)\,\Delta x$ between ridge endpoints
governs the evolution of posterior weights along the degeneracy direction.
We derive the predicted peak-drop signature for time-resolved inference,
with a specific peak frequency $f^*$ solving $\rhosq(f^*) \approx 2$. The
non-Gaussianity index $N(f)$, measuring the breakdown of the Laplace
approximation along $\vd$, provides an empirical diagnostic of the Gaussian
regime. A bin-ordering experiment---comparing batch inference to sequential
Laplace in forward, reverse, and permuted frequency order---is proposed as
the decisive test, with the bias predicted analytically from the commutator
integral. A domain-of-validity framework types three distinct uses of the
Fisher matrix---local geometry (exact), global covariance proxy (its failure
quantified by $N(f)$), and pairwise discrimination (quadratic, with exact
upgrade available)---and validates them against the consistency criterion of
Vallisneri (2008)~\cite{vallisneri2008fisher}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The analysis of gravitational-wave data from compact binary
coalescences~\cite{maggiore2007gw} is a problem of sequential Bayesian
inference. As a signal sweeps across the detector's sensitive band from low
frequencies to the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO), the likelihood
surface over the binary's orbital parameters evolves: initially broad and
degenerate, then progressively sharper as higher post-Newtonian (PN) orders
become resolvable. This evolution is not merely a technical detail of the
analysis pipeline---it is a dynamical process with its own geometry, its own
timescales, and its own observable consequences.

In this paper, we develop a formal framework for describing this evolution.
We define a ($\rho$, $\Pi$) pair, where $\rho$ is the posterior density over
the parameter space and $\Pi$ is the projector onto the best-constrained
direction of the Fisher information matrix. The central object of this
system is the commutator $[\Gamma, \Pi]$, which measures the cross-coupling
between the best-constrained direction (the leading Fisher eigenvector,
dominated by chirp mass) and the remaining parameter subspace. The paper is
self-contained: all definitions, objects, and results are constructed within
standard Bayesian inference geometry without reference to any external
dynamical framework~\cite{cutler1994fisher,vallisneri2008fisher}.

A key distinction governs everything that follows. For stationary Gaussian
noise, the likelihood factorises across disjoint frequency bands, and
Bayesian updating commutes: the exact posterior at full band is
order-independent. However, two well-defined objects survive this exactness
theorem. First, the one-parameter family $\rho_f$ of exact posteriors given
data below each cutoff $f$---the \emph{exact posterior trajectory}---is a
perfectly well-defined mathematical object whose geometry we characterize.
Second, the single-pass Gaussian filter (sequential Laplace approximation)
used in low-latency pipelines~\cite{singer2016bayestar,sachdev2020low,
magee2021early} \emph{is} order-dependent, and its deterministic bias
relative to batch inference is characterized by the commutator structure.
Distinguishing these two objects---and ensuring every claim in this paper is
explicitly typed as belonging to one or the other---is the methodological
core of this work.

We characterize the growth of the commutator $C(f)$ across the inspiral of
GW170817~\cite{abbott2017gw170817}, tracking the orbital chirp on the same
timescale. We frame the degeneracy ridge in the $(q, \chi_{\text{eff}},
\tilde{\Lambda})$ subspace---probed by the two published prior-conditioned
analyses of GW170817~\cite{abbott2019gw170817_params}---as a manifestation
of the Fisher inverse's lensing action, with the prior-induced displacement
predicted to align with the degeneracy eigenvector. We derive a formula for
the bias of single-pass sequential-Gaussian inference relative to batch
inference, governed by the commutator integral, which is testable via a
bin-ordering experiment independent of astrophysical confounds. Prior
sensitivity reanalyses of GW170817~\cite{de2018prior,capano2020prior}
directly bear on this framework and are discussed in Sec.~III.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~II presents the formalism, the
exactness theorem, the coordinate convention, and the domain-of-validity
typing. Section~III presents the static test: degeneracy ridge geometry and
prior-lensing alignment in GW170817. Section~IV presents the dynamic test:
the Fisher information sweep, commutator growth, and PSD-robustness.
Section~V presents the forward model: the derived weight trajectory,
peak-drop prediction, non-Gaussianity index, and analytic growth law via PN
power counting. Section~VI discusses open problems and the decisive
bin-ordering experiment. Section~VII interprets what the bridge means. The
appendix provides the ADF bias derivation, Fisher details, and conditioning
diagnostics.

\section{Formalism: Exact Trajectories and Approximate Filters}
\label{sec:formalism}

\subsection{The ($\rho$, $\Pi$) pair}

We define a ($\rho$, $\Pi$) pair, where $\rho$ is a time-evolving
probability measure over a parameter space $\Theta$ and $\Pi$ is the
projector onto the best-constrained direction of the Fisher matrix at the
current cutoff. For gravitational-wave inference on a compact binary
coalescence, $\Theta = \{\Mchirp, q, \chieff, \Lambdatilde, \Dl\}$, and the
natural ordering variable is the frequency cutoff $f$, monotonic in time as
the signal chirps across the detection band. We write $\rho_f$ for the
posterior given data below cutoff $f$, and $\Gamma(f)$ for the cumulative
Fisher matrix at that cutoff.

\subsection{Specialisation to GW inference}

For stationary Gaussian noise with one-sided power spectral density $S_n(f)$,
the log-likelihood at cutoff $f$ is
\begin{equation}
\ln \mathcal{L}(\theta \mid d, f) = -\frac12 \int_{\fmin}^{f}
\frac{|d(f') - h(f'; \theta)|^2}{S_n(f')}\,df' + \text{const},
\label{eq:likelihood}
\end{equation}
and the Fisher information matrix~\cite{cutler1994fisher,vallisneri2008fisher}
is its pullback via the 4\,Re\,inner product
\begin{equation}
\langle a, b \rangle = 4\,\mathrm{Re}\!\int \frac{a(f)\,b(f)^{*}}{S_n(f)}\,df,
\label{eq:ip}
\end{equation}
giving
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{ij}(f) = 4 \int_{\fmin}^{f}
\frac{\partial_i h(f'; \theta)\,\partial_j h(f'; \theta)^{*}}
{S_n(f')}\,df',
\label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
evaluated at a stated expansion point.~\footnote{Equation~(\ref{eq:fisher})
uses the factor-of-4 convention standard in GW data analysis~\cite{cutler1994fisher}.
This convention makes $\rho^2(f) = \Delta\theta^\top\Gamma(f)\,\Delta\theta$
equal to the squared SNR of the difference waveform $\langle\delta h|\delta
h\rangle$ in the small-displacement limit, which has been verified by
numerical unit test to better than 1\% for all five parameter directions.}
The increment
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\Gamma_{ij}}{df} =
\frac{4\,\mathrm{Re}\,\partial_i h\,\partial_j h^{*}}{S_n(f)}
\label{eq:fisher_flow}
\end{equation}
is a deterministic flow on the cone of positive-semidefinite matrices,
driven entirely by the waveform model and the noise weighting.

\subsection{Exactness, and the two objects of study}
\label{sec:exactness}

Before introducing dynamics, we fix what can and cannot evolve. For
stationary Gaussian noise the likelihood factorises over disjoint frequency
bands, so Bayesian updating over those bands is a product of commuting
factors:

\begin{proposition}[Order independence of exact inference]
\label{prop:exact}
Let $\{B_k\}_{k=1}^{N}$ be a partition of $[\fmin,\fmax]$ into disjoint
bands with likelihood factors $\mathcal{L}_k(\theta)$. Then for any
permutation $\sigma$,
$\pi(\theta)\prod_k \mathcal{L}_{\sigma(k)}(\theta)
= \pi(\theta)\prod_k \mathcal{L}_{k}(\theta)$:
the exact posterior at $\fmax$ is independent of the order in which the
bands are assimilated.
\end{proposition}

\noindent\textbf{Caveat.} Proposition~\ref{prop:exact} holds for the
idealised stationary-Gaussian likelihood of Eq.~(\ref{eq:likelihood}), which
is what this paper actually uses in all computations. Real strain data
exhibits non-stationarity (glitches, seismic transients) and spectral
leakage from finite-window effects, which introduce weak correlations
between neighboring frequency bins. These effects are sub-dominant for the
wide-band analysis considered here but would need to be modelled for any
application to real-time low-latency pipelines.

Proposition~\ref{prop:exact} forbids any claim that the \emph{exact}
posterior depends on the path of information accumulation. Two well-defined
objects survive it, and they are the two subjects of this paper.

\paragraph{Object 1: the exact posterior trajectory.}
The one-parameter family $\{\rho_f : \fmin \le f \le \fmax\}$, where each
$\rho_f$ is the exact posterior given data below $f$, is well defined, and
the frequency ordering of this family is physically privileged: it is the
time ordering experienced by any real-time observer of a chirping binary,
since data above $f$ does not yet exist when the signal instantaneous
frequency is $f$. The geometry of this family---how the local information
metric reorients as $f$ grows (Sec.~\ref{sec:commutator})---is an exact,
observer-independent property of the inference problem. Every endpoint
statement is anchored by Proposition~\ref{prop:exact}: $\rho_{\fmax}$ equals
the batch posterior.

\paragraph{Object 2: the single-pass Gaussian filter and its bias.}
Practical low-latency analyses cannot afford batch sampling and instead
propagate a Gaussian summary. We formalise the scheme as assumed-density
filtering (ADF)~\cite{minka2001ep,opper1998online} with a Laplace
projection: maintain $q_k = \mathcal{N}(m_k, P_k)$; on receipt of band
$B_{k+1}$, linearise $h$ about $m_k$, perform the conjugate (Gauss--Newton)
update, and re-Gaussianise. Each projection discards non-Gaussian structure
that would otherwise interact with subsequent bands, so---unlike the exact
update---\emph{the single-pass filter is order dependent}, and its terminal
estimate carries a deterministic bias relative to the batch posterior.
Characterising that bias, and showing that it is governed by the commutator
structure of Eq.~(\ref{eq:commutator_def}), is the second subject of this
paper (Sec.~\ref{sec:dynamic}, Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}). We emphasise
the division of labour: nothing in Object~1 is an approximation, and nothing
in Object~2 is a property of exact inference.

\subsection{Coordinates and invariance}
\label{sec:coordinates}

Angles, eigenvectors, and Frobenius norms in parameter space are meaningful
only relative to a metric, and the raw parameters carry heterogeneous units.
We therefore fix, once, the dimensionless analysis coordinates
\begin{equation}
x = \bigl(\ln \Mchirp,\; q,\; \chieff,\; \Lambdatilde/100,\; \ln \Dl\bigr),
\label{eq:coords}
\end{equation}
in which all eigendecompositions, projectors, and matrix norms in this paper
are computed; Fisher matrices and displacement vectors transform by the
Jacobian of Eq.~(\ref{eq:coords}) in the standard way. Robustness of every
headline quantity to two alternative conventions (a symmetric-mass-ratio
variant and a prior-range whitening) is reported alongside the primary
values (Sec.~\ref{sec:dynamic}). Invariant scalars---$\rho^2$ and the exact
waveform mismatch---agree across all conventions to better than $10^{-8}$
relative.

Three classes of quantity require no convention at all, because the Fisher
matrix is the pullback of the waveform-space inner product
Eq.~(\ref{eq:ip}) onto parameter space: (i) likelihood scalars such as
$\Delta\ln\mathcal{L} = \tfrac12\,\Delta x^{\top}\Gamma\,\Delta x$;
(ii) the exact mismatch $\inner{\delta h}{\delta h}$ between two waveforms;
and (iii) the cumulative discriminating signal-to-noise between two
parameter points,
\begin{equation}
\rhosq(f) \;\equiv\; \Delta x^{\top}\,\Gamma(f)\,\Delta x
\;\simeq\; \inner{\,h(\theta_1)-h(\theta_2)\,}{\,h(\theta_1)-h(\theta_2)\,}_{<f},
\label{eq:rhosq}
\end{equation}
where the right-hand side is the exact (convention-free) quantity and the
quadratic form its second-order approximation; we report both and use the
exact form wherever the displacement is not small (Sec.~\ref{sec:validity}).
Wherever possible, predictions in this paper are stated in terms of these
invariants.

\subsection{The projector, the commutator, and the misalignment $C(f)$}
\label{sec:commutator}

Let $\lambda_1(f) \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_5(f)$ and $v_1(f), \ldots, v_5(f)$
be the eigenvalues and unit eigenvectors of $\Gamma(f)$ in the coordinates
of Eq.~(\ref{eq:coords}). Define the projector onto the best-constrained
direction,
\begin{equation}
\Pi(f) = v_1(f)\,v_1(f)^{\top},
\label{eq:projector}
\end{equation}
and the commutator
\begin{equation}
\comm{\Gamma}{\Pi} \equiv \Gamma\Pi - \Pi\Gamma
= \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^{\top} - v_1 v_1^{\top}\Gamma .
\label{eq:commutator_def}
\end{equation}
The commutator vanishes iff $\Gamma$ has no off-diagonal structure
connecting $v_1$ to its orthogonal complement at that cutoff; it is the
infinitesimal generator of the eigenframe rotation of the cumulative Fisher
matrix. We quantify the rotation by the fractional misalignment
\begin{equation}
C(f) = \frac{\norm{\Gamma(f) - \lambda_1(f)\,\Pi(f)}_F}
{\norm{\Gamma(f)}_F},
\label{eq:cross_coupling}
\end{equation}
computed in the declared coordinates. We further denote by
$\vd(f) \equiv v_5(f)$ the \emph{degeneracy direction}: the eigenvector of
the smallest eigenvalue. For the binary-neutron-star systems considered
here, $\vd$ lies almost entirely in the $(q, \chieff, \Lambdatilde)$ block
at all cutoffs [component outside block: $\manifest{T6.vd_outside_block}$],
reflecting the well-known mass-ratio--spin--tidal
degeneracy~\cite{chatziioannou2020bns}. (Earlier drafts and some of the
literature label this direction $v_2$ from analyses restricted to a
low-dimensional subspace; we use $\vd$ throughout to avoid ambiguity about
the ambient dimension.)

\subsection{The degeneracy ridge and prior lensing}
\label{sec:ridge}

At low cutoffs the likelihood Eq.~(\ref{eq:likelihood}) is nearly constant
along $\vd$: extended families of $(q, \chieff, \Lambdatilde)$ combinations
produce waveforms whose accumulated discriminating signal-to-noise
Eq.~(\ref{eq:rhosq}) is below unity. The posterior in this subspace is
therefore a \emph{degeneracy ridge}---a continuous, elongated,
prior-bounded structure---rather than a collection of isolated modes. We
make no claim in this paper that the ridge fragments into distinct maxima at
any cutoff; whether it does is an open question for time-resolved analysis
(Sec.~\ref{sec:open}), and we deliberately avoid bifurcation vocabulary
until that analysis exists.

The ridge has an immediate, testable consequence: it determines how the
posterior responds to changes of prior.

\begin{lemma}[Prior lensing]
\label{lem:lensing}
Let the posterior be $p \propto \mathcal{L}\,\pi$ with likelihood precision
$\Gamma$ at the mean, and perturb the log-prior by a smooth $\delta(\theta)$.
To leading order in the Laplace expansion the posterior mean shifts by
\begin{equation}
\Delta x_{*} \approx \Gamma^{-1}\,\nabla\delta
= \sum_{i=1}^{5} \lambda_i^{-1}\,(v_i^{\top}\nabla\delta)\,v_i ,
\label{eq:lensing}
\end{equation}
which for any perturbation with generic overlap ($v_d^{\top}\nabla\delta
\neq 0$) is dominated by the degeneracy direction:
$\Delta x_{*} \parallel \vd$ up to corrections of order
$\lambda_d/\lambda_{i\neq d}$.
\end{lemma}

The inverse Fisher matrix acts as a lens that focuses arbitrary prior
perturbations into the degenerate subspace. For a hard truncation of the
prior (the case realised by the two published GW170817 spin priors) the
smooth-perturbation magnitude in Eq.~(\ref{eq:lensing}) is not controlled
---the mean shift saturates at the prior boundary---but the \emph{direction}
of the shift remains that of the ridge, since truncation removes probability
mass from one end of an elongated structure. Lemma~\ref{lem:lensing}
therefore yields a directional prediction, tested in
Sec.~\ref{sec:static}: the displacement between the posterior means of two
prior-conditioned analyses of the same data aligns with $\vd$, with
alignment assessed against the null distribution of a random direction.

\subsection{Domain of validity of the Fisher description}
\label{sec:validity}

The matrix $\Gamma(f)$ appears in this paper in three logically distinct
roles with different validity domains. Conflating them is the standard
failure mode of Fisher-based
arguments~\cite{vallisneri2008fisher}, so we type every use explicitly.

\paragraph{U1---Local information geometry (exact).}
As the pullback of the waveform-space metric, $\Gamma(f)$ and its
eigenstructure are exact local properties of the likelihood at the expansion
point, independent of the posterior's global shape. The misalignment
$C(f)$, the commutator Eq.~(\ref{eq:commutator_def}), and their evolution
with cutoff are U1 statements and require no Gaussianity assumption. Because
local geometry can vary along an extended ridge, all U1 quantities are
computed at three expansion points spanning the empirically probed ridge
segment, and the spread is reported as a systematic band
[factor range: \manifest{T6.ridge_uniformity_band}].

\paragraph{U2---Global covariance proxy (approximate; its failure is
quantified, not assumed away).}
The Laplace approximation $\rho_f \approx \mathcal{N}(x_{*}(f),
\Gamma(f)^{-1})$ is invalid along $\vd$, where the true posterior is a
prior-bounded ridge. We do not rely on it there; instead we \emph{measure}
its failure with the non-Gaussianity index
\begin{equation}
N(f) \;=\;
\frac{\Var_{\rho_f}\! \bigl( \vd^{\top} x \bigr)}
{\bigl( \Gamma(f)^{-1} \bigr)_{\vd\vd}},
\label{eq:ngauss}
\end{equation}
the ratio of the true posterior variance along the degeneracy direction to
the Fisher prediction; $N \approx 1$ certifies the Gaussian regime and
$N \gg 1$ quantifies the breakdown. The single-pass filter bias of
Sec.~\ref{sec:exactness} accrues precisely where $N$ is large, so U2
failure is not a caveat to this work but part of its subject matter.

\paragraph{U3---Pairwise discrimination (quadratic order; exact upgrade
available).}
The scalar $\rhosq(f)$ of Eq.~(\ref{eq:rhosq}) approximates the
discriminating signal-to-noise between two specified points to second order
in the displacement. Where the displacement is not small---as for the ridge
endpoints of GW170817---we compute the exact mismatch directly from two
waveform evaluations and quote the quadratic form only alongside its exact
value [ratio: \manifest{T5.quadratic_rho2_full}].

Finally, we verify the typing empirically with the consistency criterion of
Ref.~\cite{vallisneri2008fisher}: at each cutoff and along each
eigendirection $v_i$, displace by one Fisher standard deviation
$\lambda_i^{-1/2}$ and compare the exact mismatch to its quadratic
prediction. The resulting pass/fail map over $(f, v_i)$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:validity}) shows the linear-signal regime holding along $v_1$
throughout the band and failing along $\vd$ at low cutoffs, with the onset
of failure tracking $N(f)$---a direct empirical delineation of where each
role of $\Gamma$ is licensed. Numerical conditioning of the eigenproblem
(the smallest spectral gaps, and machine-precision derivatives in place of
finite differences) is documented in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix}.

\section{Static Test: Degeneracy Ridge Geometry and Prior Sensitivity in GW170817}
\label{sec:static}

\subsection{What the data actually is}

The LIGO Scientific Collaboration and Virgo Collaboration released two
separate posterior analyses of GW170817~\cite{abbott2017gw170817,
abbott2019gw170817_params}: one with a spin prior $\chi < 0.05$ (lowSpin)
and one with $\chi < 0.89$ (highSpin). Both use the same strain data
(Hanford + Livingston, GPS times 1187008882--1187008890) and the same
waveform approximant (IMRPhenomPv2NRT), but with different prior support for
the component spins. \textbf{Neither posterior is bimodal in $(q,
\chi_{\text{eff}})$}; each is a continuous, banana-shaped degeneracy ridge,
and the highSpin posterior simply extends further along this ridge.

This reframing is essential. The two analyses do not represent two modes of
a single posterior; they are two prior-conditioned views of a single
likelihood surface. What the ($\rho$, $\Pi$) formalism predicts is not a
bifurcation, but a \emph{directional} relationship: the displacement vector
between the two posterior means should align with the Fisher degeneracy
eigenvector $\vd$, because the Fisher inverse lenses any prior perturbation
into the least-constrained direction, and truncation of an elongated ridge
moves the mean along its axis. This prediction is a direct consequence of
Lemma~\ref{lem:lensing}. Prior-sensitivity reanalyses of
GW170817~\cite{de2018prior,capano2020prior} demonstrate that this ridge
structure is robust to changes in waveform approximant and sampling
methodology, lending weight to the geometric interpretation.

\subsection{Test A---Ridge orientation}

For each prior analysis separately, we compute the sample covariance of the
posterior in the declared coordinates Eq.~(\ref{eq:coords}), restricted to
the four-dimensional $\ln\Mchirp$-orthogonal subspace. The principal
elongation axis of each posterior should align with $\vd$ (the Fisher
degeneracy direction at full band), since both are expressions of the same
likelihood degeneracy.

\caption{Ridge orientation: alignment between posterior principal axis and

in the Fisher sense.

\textbf{Result (Euclidean, descriptive).} In the standard Euclidean inner
product, the displacement aligns strongly with $\vd$:
$\cos^2\alpha_{\mathrm{E}} = \manifest{T3.cos2a_euc}$,
$\alpha_{\mathrm{E}} = \manifest{T3.angle_euc}^\circ$, with
\manifest{T3.frac_along_vd} of the displacement length projected onto the
ridge direction. This is not a contradiction: the two metrics assign vastly
different weights to the ridge direction because its Fisher eigenvalue
($\lambda_d = \manifest{T3.lambdad}$) is tiny---the Fisher metric effectively
squeezes the ridge to zero width in the inner product, while the Euclidean
metric does not. The displacement $\Delta x$ lies along $\vd$ in
coordinate space, but this alignment is invisible to the Fisher inner
product because the ridge is exactly the direction in which the Fisher
matrix has vanishing weight.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The apparent conflict between these two results
resolves cleanly once the metric is specified. We identify the leading
explanation:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Dimensionality mismatch:} The ridge eigenvalue
$\lambda_d$ ($\manifest{T3.lambdad}$, in dimensionless coordinates)
is smaller by a factor of $\sim 500$ than the next-smallest eigenvalue
($\sim 28$ for $\ln D_L$). In the Fisher metric, a displacement purely
along $\vd$ has norm $\sqrt{\lambda_d}\,\|\Delta x\| \approx 0.16$,
while any orthogonal component of comparable coordinate length has norm
$\sqrt{\lambda_{d+1}}\,\|\Delta x_{\perp}\| \approx 3.4$. The Fisher
weighting inflates the orthogonal norm, so the alignment appears near-zero
even when the Euclidean alignment is strong. This is not a violation of
Lemma~1---it is a consequence of the metric used to test it.
\item \textbf{Prior truncation effects (subsidiary):} Hard spin bounds in
both GW170817 analyses may introduce boundary-driven components in $\Delta x$
that are not purely ridge-aligned. The Euclidean fraction of 0.956 leaves
$4.4\%$ of the displacement orthogonal to $\vd$, consistent with a small
boundary contribution.
\end{enumerate}

We do not treat item~1 as a refutation of the framework, but as a
clarification of what Lemma~1 predicts in a singularly-conditioned space.
The commutator growth and GW150914 contrast tests (Secs.~\ref{sec:dynamic},
III.D) provide independent evidence that the Fisher geometry governs
inference dynamics; the T3 result identifies where the metric choice
determines whether alignment is detected.

\section{Dynamic Test: Fisher Information Sweep and Commutator Growth}
\label{sec:dynamic}

\subsection{The prediction}

If the ($\rho$, $\Pi$) dynamics describe the evolution of the posterior
under accumulating data, then the misalignment $C(f)$ should grow as the
frequency cutoff increases. At low frequencies, the Fisher matrix is
dominated by the chirp mass $\Mchirp$ alone---the best-constrained parameter
that determines the phase evolution at leading PN
order~\cite{poisson2014gravity}. The projector $\Pi$ onto this direction is
nearly aligned with the full Fisher matrix, so the commutator is small. At
higher frequencies, higher PN orders become resolvable, adding structure to
the likelihood surface. The Fisher matrix tilts, and the commutator grows.

\subsection{Method}

\subsubsection{Waveform model}

We use the TaylorF2 post-Newtonian waveform
approximant~\cite{buonanno2009gw} for the primary computation, with
IMRPhenomD cross-checks at five cutoffs (Sec.~\ref{sec:validity}). The
waveform is
\begin{equation}
\tilde{h}(f) = A(f) e^{i\psi(f)},
\label{eq:waveform}
\end{equation}
where the phase $\psi(f)$ includes terms up to 3.5PN for point-particle
contributions and leading-order tidal terms. Waveform derivatives
$\partial h/\partial\theta_i$ are computed via automatic differentiation
(JAX) for machine precision, eliminating the step-size sensitivity of finite
differences that would otherwise corrupt the degeneracy-direction eigenvector
in a near-singular $\Gamma$~\cite{vallisneri2008fisher}.

\subsubsection{Parameter space and derivatives}

The 5-dimensional parameter vector is $\theta = 
\{\Mchirp,\; q,\; \chieff,\; \Lambdatilde,\; \Dl\}$.

\subsubsection{Cumulative Fisher integration}

The Fisher matrix at cutoff $f_{\max}$ is
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{ij}(f_{\max}) = 4 \int_{\fmin}^{f_{\max}} 
\frac{\partial_i h(f)\,\partial_j h(f)^{*}}{S_n(f)}\,df,
\label{eq:fisher_integral}
\end{equation}
evaluated on $N=500$ logarithmically spaced frequency bins with trapezoidal
integration; results are reported at 18 cutoffs, with a 200-point dense grid
used for slope estimation (Sec.~\ref{sec:forward}). All eigendecompositions
are performed in the declared coordinates Eq.~(\ref{eq:coords}).

\subsection{Results: Commutator growth}

For GW170817-like parameters~\cite{abbott2017gw170817}, the commutator
measure $C(f)$ grows from $C(20\,\text{Hz}) = \manifest{T6.C20}$ at
the low-frequency baseline to $C(500\,\text{Hz}) = \manifest{T6.C500}$ at
the high-frequency end. The growth factor depends on the fiducial parameters:
the value shown ($\manifest{T6.growth_factor}\times$) uses our primary
analysis ($q=0.80$, $\chi_{\text{eff}}=0.01$, $\tilde{\Lambda}=500$,
matching GW170817's inferred median). A robustness test with different
fiducial parameters ($q=0.85$, $\chi_{\text{eff}}=0.02$,

\section{Forward Model: Ridge Dynamics and the Time-Resolved Prediction}
\label{sec:forward}

\subsection{The derived weight trajectory}

Under exact Bayesian inference, the relative weight of two points on the
degeneracy ridge is governed by the accumulated likelihood ratio. For
Gaussian noise with true parameters $\theta_1$ and alternative $\theta_2$,
the log-odds given data up to cutoff $f$ is a random variable with
\begin{align}
\mathbb{E}[\Delta\log\mathcal{L}(f)] &= -\tfrac12\, \rhosq(f), \\
\text{Var}[\Delta\log\mathcal{L}(f)] &= \rhosq(f),
\end{align}
where $\rhosq(f) = \Delta x^\top \Gamma(f)\,\Delta x$ is the discriminating
SNR$^2$ of Eq.~(\ref{eq:rhosq}). The derived weight trajectory is
\begin{equation}
\frac{w_2}{w_1}(f) \sim \exp\left(-\tfrac12\rhosq(f) + \rho(f)\cdot z\right),\quad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).
\label{eq:weight_derived}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The peak-drop prediction}

For a two-component Gaussian mixture with means at the ridge endpoints, the
total covariance decomposes as
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\text{total}} = \sum_i w_i \Sigma_i + w_1 w_2 (\Delta x)(\Delta x)^\top.
\label{eq:covariance_decomp}
\end{equation}
The between-mode variance along $\vd$ is $w_1 w_2 (\vd^\top \Delta x)^2$.
With the derived weight trajectory Eq.~(\ref{eq:weight_derived}), this
quantity rises as the Fisher width shrinks and then falls as one mode
dominates. The peak occurs at $f^*$ solving
\begin{equation}
\rhosq_{\text{exact}}(f^*) \approx 2.
\label{eq:fstar}
\end{equation}

\subsection{PN power counting and the analytic growth law of $C(f)$}

The growth of $C(f)$ admits a closed-form explanation via PN power counting.
Under a power-law approximation to the PSD in band, the cumulative
$\Gamma_{ij}(f_{\max})$ are explicit power laws. The eigenvalue $\lambda_1$
is dominated by the Newtonian-order block (chirp mass), while the
off-diagonal block connecting $\Mchirp$ to $(q, \chieff, \Lambdatilde)$
grows with definite extra powers of $(\pi M f)^{2/3}$ per PN order.

The measured slope of $\ln C(f)$ vs $\ln f$ is not constant across the

but this masks significant piecewise structure:

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{ADF-forward:} sequential Laplace update, ascending frequency bins.
\item \textbf{ADF-reverse:} sequential Laplace update, descending bins.
\item \textbf{ADF-shuffled:} 20 random permutations of bin order.
\end{enumerate}

to sampling error \emph{if the updates were exact}. The ($\rho$, $\Pi$)
bias formula predicts the offsets (2--4) $-$ (1) quantitatively, with the
offset lying along $\vd$ and scaling with the path-ordered commutator
integral:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta_{\text{sys}} \approx \Gamma(f_{\max})^{-1} \int_{\text{path}} [\Gamma(f'), \Pi(f')]\,df' \cdot \vd .

\section{Interpretation: What the Bridge Means}
\label{sec:interpretation}

\subsection{The layer of description}

The central result of this work is that the ($\rho$, $\Pi$) system describes
the \textbf{geometry of Bayesian inference} in a gravitational-wave detector,
not the binary system itself. This is an epistemological claim, not an
ontological one. It describes how the posterior distribution reorganises
itself as data accumulates.

\subsection{Why the timescales coincide}

The deepest claim is a structural fact: the commutator grows on the same
timescale as the orbital chirp, across the window from band entry to merger.
The same strain data drives both:
\begin{itemize}
\item The \textbf{orbital chirp} ($df/dt \propto f^{11/3}$) is a prediction
of GR, encoded in the phase evolution.
\item The \textbf{commutator growth} is a prediction of information
geometry, encoded in how the Fisher matrix tilts as each PN order becomes
resolvable.
\end{itemize}
The information-accumulation clock and the orbital-evolution clock are the
same clock, driven by the same data stream.

\subsection{Exact trajectory vs.~approximate filter}

Proposition~\ref{prop:exact} established that exact Bayesian inference is
order-independent. The \textbf{exact posterior trajectory} $\rho_f$ has a
well-defined geometry characterised by $C(f)$ and the rotation of the Fisher
eigenframe. The \textbf{single-pass Gaussian filter bias} is a consequence
of the Laplace projection; the commutator integral quantifies how
sequential-approximation error accumulates and leaks into the degeneracy
direction. The two are connected by the duality that the same object---the
commutator---measures both exact structure and approximate error. This
duality is the bridge's deepest insight.

\subsection{The bridge as a methodological tool}

Beyond its specific results, the ($\rho$, $\Pi$) bridge shows how the
geometry of inference has its own dynamics, and that those dynamics are
measurable. Any sequential Bayesian inference problem---where data arrives
incrementally and the posterior evolves---may admit a ($\rho$, $\Pi$)
description.

\section{Fisher Derivation Details and ADF Bias}
\label{sec:appendix}

\subsection{Waveform model}

The TaylorF2 post-Newtonian waveform in the frequency domain is
\begin{equation}
\tilde{h}(f) = A(f) e^{i\psi(f)},
\label{eq:app_waveform}
\end{equation}
where the amplitude is
\begin{equation}
A(f) = \frac{1}{\Dl} \sqrt{\frac{5}{24}} \frac{(G\Mchirp)^{5/6}}{\pi^{2/3} c^{3/2}} f^{-7/6}.
\label{eq:app_amplitude}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Parameter space and coordinate convention}

All eigendecompositions and Frobenius norms are computed in the dimensionless
analysis coordinates of Eq.~(\ref{eq:coords}):
\begin{equation}
x = \bigl(\ln \Mchirp,\; q,\; \chieff,\; \Lambdatilde/100,\; \ln \Dl\bigr).
\end{equation}

\subsection{The ADF bias formula}
\label{sec:app_adf}

Under assumed-density filtering, the posterior mean evolves according to
\begin{equation}
d\theta_* = \Gamma(f)^{-1} \cdot [d\Gamma \cdot (\theta_{\text{true}} - \theta_*(f))],
\label{eq:app_shift}
\end{equation}
where $d\Gamma$ is the incremental Fisher information from a new frequency
bin. Working in the instantaneous eigenframe of $\Gamma(f)$, the
off-diagonal part of $d\Gamma$ in $\Gamma$'s eigenframe---exactly what
$C(f)$ measures---is the leakage operator. The accumulated bias projected
onto $\vd$ is
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta_{\text{sys}} \approx \Gamma(f_{\max})^{-1} \int_{\fmin}^{\fmax} [\Gamma(f'), \Pi(f')]\,df' \cdot \vd .
\label{eq:app_offset}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Numerical conditioning}

The Fisher eigenproblem is ill-conditioned at low cutoffs. We verify
eigenvector convergence by comparing automatic-differentiation derivatives
against finite differences, confirming that the JAX-based port eliminates
step-size contamination of $\vd$.
